Pedagogy and Use of Adapted Switches in the School Context

Pedagogy Using Switches: Multiple Choice

The use of adapted toys, environmental controllers, or digital activities—like those offered on this site—provides accessible learning experiences for a large proportion of students with multiple disabilities. These activities fit well within their zone of proximal development, promoting engagement and exploration through action.

However, for some students, these approaches may eventually become redundant and keep them in a static equilibrium: they understand the process, but are no longer challenged to progress. In such cases, it becomes necessary to go beyond simple cause and effect by using switches and other adapted technologies to open up new learning possibilities, particularly in terms of self-determination and decision-making.

In the digital realm, the introduction of two-switch systems (one for scrolling and one for selecting) or automatic scanning with a single switch increases interaction opportunities and significantly enriches the range of possible exchanges. However, as Ian Bean notes in his training, learning scanning or multiple-choice activities requires high cognitive abilities and involves relatively abstract mental processes. Thus, it is often a long and demanding process, but for some students it may be the only real means to achieve self-determination. That is why it is recommended to persevere and maintain stimulating, adapted learning contexts, even when progress seems slow or subtle.

When to Transition to Multiple Choice?

Everyone can benefit from action/reaction activities using a switch. Thanks to technological advances, even the subtlest movements can be detected and used to interact with various devices. However, accessing multiple-choice activities represents a higher level of complexity and involves certain prerequisites related to the student's abilities.

It is entirely appropriate to try this type of activity with any student, even if their profile does not perfectly match expectations. However, it is important to consider the opportunity cost: investing time, energy, and resources in a learning process that may not fall within the student's zone of proximal development can divert attention from more accessible and meaningful experiences.

For students with multiple disabilities, profiles are often difficult to define precisely, and skill assessment is a real challenge. This is why it can be useful to refer to certain indicators to guide reflection. The following elements may suggest that a student—and their educational environment—could benefit from the gradual introduction of multiple-choice activities using switches.

Effectiveness in a Cause-and-Effect Activity Using a Switch

This factor is probably the easiest to observe and evaluate since it is based on behavioral indicators visible during adapted activities. Although the process remains subjective, careful observation of the student in different situations can reveal signs that they are ready to progress to multiple-choice tasks.

The key is to assess whether the student appears to understand the cause-and-effect relationship, enjoys the activity, and acts with a certain fluidity. It is also important to note if they react quickly, show progress compared to a previous level, and if the skill they employ remains stable over time. These observations, though subjective, provide a set of indicators that, taken together, help determine if the student is ready for more complex interactions.

More Varied Interests

Expressing interests and preferences is an important prerequisite for introducing multiple-choice activities. Although sometimes less obvious, this factor plays a key role in learning. For children with multiple disabilities, a diversity of interests signifies progress in interacting with their environment, making the act of choosing more meaningful.

A clear interest helps the student understand the choice: they learn to link their action to a result. Conversely, if their reactions remain random, they may struggle to grasp the impact of their decisions. The more motivating the options, the more effective the learning. A too limited repertoire of interests may restrict engagement and lead to mechanical rather than intentional choices.

The diversity of preferences also reinforces active engagement: a student who chooses between two distinct options develops the ability to differentiate, anticipate, and express an intention. Thus, it both contributes to the initiation of this learning process and is reinforced by it. Multiple-choice activities are likely one of the best ways to allow students to diversify their interests and for caregivers to discover them.

Attentional Processes

Sustained and selective attention are key attentional processes in learning multiple-choice activities with a switch. They enable the student to maintain engagement and make deliberate choices. Sustained attention is essential for repeating a motor gesture and remaining focused on the presented options long enough to select one thoughtfully. Without this ability, the student may press the switch at random without understanding the consequences. Gradual development of these skills allows for increasing the complexity of choices—from two to three and beyond—thus making the experience richer and more meaningful.

Selective attention, on the other hand, enables the filtering of distractions and focusing solely on the relevant option. A student with weak selective attention may be easily drawn to irrelevant stimuli such as lights or ambient noise, which can hinder decision-making. A lack of focus may also lead to errors in scanning or multiple-choice tasks, giving the impression that the student does not understand the exercise when, in fact, they are having difficulty ignoring extraneous elements.

Before introducing multiple-choice or scanning tasks, it is important to ensure that the student can maintain attention long enough to analyze the available options, can ignore distractions (to some extent), and focus on their goal, as well as follow a sequence to select the appropriate option. Working on these aspects of attention could help increase the effectiveness of decision-making.

Interaction with Diverse Objects

This is an extension of developing interest in various stimuli and improving attentional skills in a more concrete and observable way. When a student is in a situation where multiple elements are available at once—such as toys in front of them, a sensory bin with various textures, or a range of structured activities—they must be able to navigate among these options, both motorically and cognitively.

This means that they must coordinate their gaze, attention, and, when possible, their movements to explore, compare, and select one element among many. This ability reflects the capacity to juggle multiple pieces of information simultaneously, a skill that is useful to observe in the student in this context.

Cognition

It can be difficult to obtain precise information on the cognitive potential of students with multiple disabilities. Some assessments may offer clues, provided they are interpreted with caution. Normative tools, such as the Bayley or ABAS, can have significant limitations in this population—especially due to floor effects and a lack of sensitivity to complex profiles. Nonetheless, a careful reading of these results may suggest a reasonable likelihood of success in learning multiple-choice tasks.

For example, if a student scores very low across all scales—especially in cognitive and motor areas—it is likely that learning this type of activity exceeds their current developmental zone. Conversely, a profile with a bit more variation, without a generalized floor effect, may indicate that there are sufficient foundations to begin progressing. The same applies to assessments related to the educational program, whether it is the PÉDIP or another. It is important to note that this is not an absolute criterion or a rigid filter, but rather one factor among many to consider when evaluating potential.

Other executive function capacities can also guide the reflection. For example, working memory enables the student to retain an option already seen during a visual scan and to wait for it to reappear.

Understanding "Timing"

This prerequisite is particularly important for scanning activities, where the student must wait for the right moment to act. It also reflects a significant cognitive ability: if the student begins to understand the concept of sequence and timing, this may indicate that they are ready for more complex activities, whether through scanning or using two switches.

The student must perceive that there is a precise window of opportunity in which their action has an effect. Unlike activities where the switch can be activated at any time, here the action requires anticipation, waiting, and a synchronized response. This presupposes a certain understanding of time and the sequence of events.

This type of skill allows the student to adjust their behavior based on temporal cues, which promotes better adaptation to the environment. It also represents an important step toward more intentional participation, related to self-determination.

Visual Tracking

To interact effectively with multiple-choice options or a scanning system, the student must be able to visually follow various options and fix their attention on each long enough to analyze them. This ability relies on two skills: visual tracking, which allows following a moving element, and visual fixation, which helps stabilize the gaze on a specific point.

Note that visual tracking typically proceeds from left to right. The vast majority of software and devices using choice or scanning functions—including those on this site—follow this direction. Culturally, at least in the West, this is the direction in which a logical sequence is followed. It is therefore important to keep this in mind during preparatory activities and assessments.

Within scanning, the student must be able to follow a fluid visual sweep and identify when an option becomes available. A choppy visual tracking or difficulty in fixing the gaze can lead to random selections and hinder understanding of the process.

These skills are essential for enabling the student to better interact with their environment, adapt to the visual sequences that structure certain activities, and act more intentionally when choosing between several options.

Organizational Considerations

To facilitate these learning processes, access to digital activities is often essential. For cause-and-effect tasks, adapted toys or stimuli connected to an environmental controller may suffice. However, once it comes to multiple-choice activities, the material requirements increase: at least two switches, a computer, and an adapter to connect the switches to the computer are needed.

This level of equipment, which might seem minimal for one student, quickly becomes important on a class-wide scale. Having multiple computers and adapters would be optimal to allow several students to practice regularly, which is not always possible in every school environment. Therefore, it is an accessibility issue to consider in educational planning.

Two Paths: Scanning or Two Switches (How to Choose)

Once it is established that a student could benefit from multiple-choice pedagogical activities, based on their profile, motor and cognitive abilities, as well as the available resources in their environment, it becomes necessary to determine the most appropriate access method. These activities can be performed using one or two switches.

With a single switch, the scanning mode is used: options scroll automatically on the screen, and the student must press at the opportune moment—when the desired option is highlighted—to select it. With two switches, the student can actively navigate from one option to another using the first switch, then select with the second. This configuration generally requires two distinct movements, ideally performed with two well-differentiated parts of the body, since placing the two switches too close together may demand significant motor effort. The choice of access mode must therefore be individualized, taking into account the student's strengths and limitations.

Criteria for Determining the Most Suitable Access Mode

The key element is motor ability. If the student can reliably move only one part of their body, scanning is preferable since it requires just one movement. Conversely, if the student can differentiate and coordinate two distinct movements, navigating with two switches offers better control over the choices.

Reaction time is another determining factor. Scanning imposes a time constraint where the student must react at the right moment when the desired option appears. If their response time is too slow or unpredictable, navigation with two switches allows them to proceed at their own pace without the fear of missing an opportunity.

The ability to visually follow a sweep is also important. Scanning requires strong visual tracking and constant attention to anticipate and select the option at the right moment. If the student struggles to follow a fluid movement or distinguish among several scrolling options, using two switches offers more direct control and reduces visual effort.

The cognitive load varies depending on the mode chosen. Scanning requires quickly analyzing each option as it appears, while navigating with two switches allows the student more time to process the information before selecting. If the student struggles to manage multiple stimuli in rapid succession, active navigation with two switches can provide better access to the choices.

Physical effort and fatigue must also be considered. Scanning is based on a single, repetitive movement that may be easier to sustain, whereas using two switches demands more sustained coordination and greater motor effort. The type of switch can alleviate this burden, but cannot eliminate it entirely.

Finally, the student's experience and preferences remain essential. It is possible that, for reasons not fully understood, a student may respond better to one access method over another. Trying both modes—with adjustments to pace and parameters—can help determine which is best suited to their profile. The goal is to ensure smooth and effective participation, taking into account each student's specific needs.

Organizational considerations also play a role in choosing the access mode, especially in situations where no clear motor or cognitive criterion exists. Using multiple switches requires not only their availability but also the necessary supports and installation mechanisms, which can be a material and logistical constraint. However, teaching navigation with two switches is often simpler to structure than scanning. It is easier to introduce the concept through activities using several adapted toys or multiple stimuli connected to an environmental controller, which helps generalize learning in various contexts. In contrast, scanning relies on specialized software and does not naturally translate into the physical environment, making its instruction more specific and abstract. These practical aspects should be considered to ensure an effective setup that is adapted to available resources, while prioritizing the student’s needs and profile.

Progression in Multiple-Choice Activities

Learning to use switches for multiple-choice activities—whether with two switches or in scanning mode—is not acquired automatically. It is based on a progressive journey, composed of successive small steps, much like any other complex learning process: reading, counting, communicating, etc. This is especially true for students with multiple disabilities, whose development may span several years with occasional periods of stagnation.

In this perspective, a conceptual map is proposed here to illustrate a logical and structured progression that a student might follow to develop skills related to choice activities. This progression is neither rigid nor linear: some students may skip steps, slow down on others, or progress and regress depending on context and timing. Note that several online resources in English offer such a developmental model—see the references at the end of this text.

This representation is intended primarily as a flexible and adaptable reference, to be used with professional discernment. It should be interpreted in light of each student's unique profile, taking into account their pace, strengths, interests, and potential for progress.

Cause and Effect:

As mentioned earlier, learning multiple-choice activities relies on a certain understanding of the cause-and-effect concept. However, there is no absolute criterion to determine if this understanding is sufficiently acquired by the student. Rather, continuous observation, combined with the professional judgment of caregivers and a solid understanding of the student's profile, allows for evaluating their readiness to progress to this mode.

This level is rich in accessible activities, both digital and concrete. It is perhaps one of the simplest aspects to integrate into daily life, given the abundance of available adapted equipment and resources. To further explore the possibilities associated with using switches at this level, you can refer to the text on learning with switches, which delves into the topic in greater depth: LINK.

Two Choices:

This simply involves presenting two choices to the student and allowing them to make a selection. To better simulate the use of two switches, it is recommended to arrange objects in a manner similar to the usual placement of the switches—one slightly to the right and one to the left, both within the student's visual and motor field. This arrangement promotes an association between the chosen object and the action that would be performed on a real switch. When the student makes a choice, it is essential to provide rapid and clear feedback to reinforce the intent of the selection and solidify the cause-and-effect link.

Two Choices (Digital):

On this site, this refers to the two-choice option in the multiple-choice module. The left option is triggered by the first switch (space) and the right option by the second switch (enter). The space travel activity in the various games section also works on this stage. On Ian Bean’s site, there are several activities in the "This or That" section that follow a very similar principle (https://www.ianbean.co.uk/senict-scanning/).

Scrolling Multiple Choice

At this stage, differentiated functions are introduced for the two switches: one scrolls through the options while the other selects among them. The choices are presented one at a time and change when the student presses the switch. This type of activity reduces the attentional load, as the student only has to process one piece of information at a time. On this site, it is known as the sequential choice mode; on Ian Bean’s site, it is referred to as Flashcards.

However, this mode introduces an additional level of abstraction compared to simultaneously presented choices. The student must understand that the desired option might appear later, even if it is not currently visible. This requires a certain understanding of object permanence.

The caregiver can support learning by modeling the expected behavior, providing an explicit demonstration, or even sharing control—for example, by leaving one switch for the student and handling the other (for scrolling or selection) based on observed reactions. The goal is for the student to develop a clear understanding that the two actions (scrolling and selecting) are distinct and produce different effects, thus laying the foundation for independent use of a two-switch system.

Multiple Choice on the Same Page

This mode involves presenting several choices simultaneously on one screen. On this site, it is the default option in the multiple-choice module; on Ian Bean’s site, these are known as multiple-choice activities. Unlike the sequential mode, where the student processes one piece of information at a time, here they must analyze several options in parallel, which significantly increases complexity both cognitively and visually.

The student uses one switch to navigate through the options—generally following a left-to-right, then top-to-bottom sweep—and another switch to select the desired option at the right moment. Motorically, the student must also be able to sustain engagement until the desired option is reached, without losing interest due to the higher number of stimuli. For many students, this stage may represent a transition point to more functional activities. As always, it is essential to tailor the number of choices to the student’s profile and favor a gradual progression, for example, by starting with two or three options.

Visual Scanning Without Digital Tools

It is less straightforward to prepare a student for scanning, since this access mode is based on an abstract mechanism for which there is no real toy or technology that concretely simulates the principle. Therefore, the learning process must rely on relational situations and meaningful everyday contexts, with the active support of a caregiver.

For example, when choosing a snack, it is possible to present two or three options sequentially, carefully observing the student’s reaction to each proposition. This type of presentation reproduces—albeit in a simplified form—the principle of a choice that “scrolls” over time rather than being offered simultaneously. The same principle can be applied in different routines. For instance, one might hum a few words of a nursery rhyme, wait for a reaction, then offer another, and so on until a clear response is observed. The adult can then linger longer on the chosen rhyme.

Finally, when several choices are presented simultaneously—whether for objects, activities, or snacks—the student may also be invited to look toward what they prefer, supported by verbal or gestural guidance. If the student demonstrates a clear understanding of these sequential situations, and is capable of waiting, identifying, and reacting to a specific option, they are likely ready to access assisted scanning activities using a technological tool.

Sequential Scanning

This activity is based on the same principle as scrolling choices, except that the scrolling occurs automatically, without the student’s intervention. On this site, this option is called sequential scanning. An important variable in this mode is the presentation delay for each option: by default, it is set to 10 seconds. This parameter must be adjusted based on the student’s profile—a pace that is too slow may lead to loss of interest or impatience, while one that is too fast may prevent the student from reacting in time, causing frustration.

When introducing this activity, the student may be allowed free exploration or be guided more actively according to their needs. It is important to remember that the principle of this mode is abstract and generally may not be immediately understood. The student will go through a trial-and-error phase to establish the link between the displayed option, the moment of switch activation, and its resulting effect. This exploratory period is normal as it allows the student to gradually grasp the basics of scanning mode navigation.

Scan

This is the standard scanning mode, in which several options are displayed simultaneously on one page. On our page, this is known as the scan option. The student can see all available choices, while a visual indicator—whose appearance varies depending on the software or device—sequentially highlights the active option. When the desired option is highlighted, the student can press the switch to select it.

This mode allows the student to view all possibilities while practicing selection based on a temporal cue, which is an important step toward functional use of communication or interaction tools. As with two-switch choices, it is advisable to start with two or three options and gradually increase the number of choices as the student becomes more comfortable.

Increasing the Number of Choices

At this stage, the two approaches—whether using two switches or scanning—converge. The student should have acquired a basic understanding of how choice activities work and be able to select among several options using the mode best suited to them. The next step is to gradually increase the number of choices offered, moving from two or three to four, five, six, or more, depending on the student’s abilities.

This change represents a new challenge, both in terms of attention and perception. The more choices there are, the more the student must maintain attention and be able to discriminate between several elements. This can become difficult if the images are too similar, if there is too much information, or if the presentation pace is too fast.

In some cases, it may be appropriate to introduce errorless activities—where all choices lead to a positive outcome—to preserve motivation and encourage participation. At this stage, once technical mastery is achieved, one can also begin to formulate instructions with an expected response (e.g., "Choose Taylor Swift's song" or "Select the red square") to engage the student in more targeted comprehension or communication activities.

Functional and Meaningful Use of Choices

When the student is able to make choices intentionally, consistently, and fluidly, activities can take on a more advanced, both communicative and functional, role in achieving their developmental objectives. It is no longer just about expressing a preference or exploring a reaction, but about transmitting information, responding to instructions, formulating a specific request, or interacting with their environment.

For example, the student may use a multiple-choice activity to indicate what they wish to do, select an activity, or even express a need in a concrete situation. This stage marks a transition toward more complex uses of switches, often linked to alternative and augmented communication (AAC) tools or environmental control.

At this stage, it becomes essential that the choices offered are meaningful and adapted to the student's daily reality. This goes beyond the scope of the activities offered on this site to enter contexts more integrated with real life, where choice becomes a true means of action and self-determination.

Best Practices

Questions

These activities are not intended to yield an objectively correct answer, but rather to give the student the opportunity to express a preference or make a personal choice. There is no right or wrong answer, and the pedagogical intent should not be geared toward performance evaluation. In this sense, it would be inappropriate to issue instructions like "Find X" or "Choose Y" expecting a correct selection.

False Choices

In the early stages, it may be appropriate to include a false choice, such as a static option available on our page, to assess the student’s level of understanding. If this non-meaningful option is frequently selected, it may indicate that the choices are made at random, suggesting that the selection is not yet fully intentional or reliable. In such cases, it is recommended to return to exploratory activities and better ascertain the student’s interests in different contexts. By ensuring that the options provided are truly motivating and meaningful to the student, the chances of eliciting authentic participation and establishing a solid foundation for more complex choices are increased. This can also be a means of testing the student’s sustained attention (e.g., if they begin to select the false choice after 15 or 20 minutes, it might be due to fatigue).

Motivation, Meaning, and Opportunity

These elements are detailed in our section on switches for cause and effect, but in summary, they are the three concepts proposed by Ian Bean, a specialist renowned for his expertise in this field. Keeping these three prerequisites in mind and using them as part of a pedagogical reflection is an excellent way to validate the relevance of the activities offered. It also helps ensure that the student progresses in a direction that is consistent with their needs and developmental level. By revisiting them regularly, they become concrete benchmarks for guiding educational support.

Annex

Suggestions for Exercises Related to Prerequisites

🔹Sustained Attention: Press and Observe

An adapted toy or a stimulus connected to an environmental controller is placed in front of the student. A switch is positioned in an accessible location, allowing the stimulus to be activated with a simple press. As soon as the student presses, the object activates: a light turns on, a melody starts, a toy moves or vibrates.

The effect occurs immediately after activation and lasts for a few seconds. During this time, the student may fix their gaze on the stimulus, listen to the sound, or follow the movement. When the effect ceases, various reactions may be observed: maintaining attention on the object, looking for another activation, or quickly turning away.

The goal is to measure how long the student maintains attention on the stimulus after activation. The activity can be repeated several times with different types of adapted toys to identify which stimuli generate the most engagement. By using a favored stimulus, it is possible to gradually increase the duration of attention—a metric that can be quantified to track the student’s progress over time.

This is a simple activity. The only difference here is that the emphasis is on the duration of attention and the attentional response (tracking or disinterest) after the stimulus ceases, rather than on the act of pressing itself, which allows analysis of the student’s ability to concentrate on a given stimulus.

Timing: Online Activities

The POV and videoclip activities on this site all have a "time limit" mode that determines, in seconds, how long a student can activate the activity. A time indicator image is displayed and then disappears. If the student presses while it is hidden (i.e., out of time), the stimulus is not activated. This is a simple way to evaluate the student’s reaction time and work on the concept of timing.

🔹Visual Tracking: Follow the Visual Path

This activity invites the student to visually follow an object or pattern moving slowly across the screen along a predetermined path. It is also possible to pause the stimulus to see if the student continues to watch it when still. This exercise is available in the pedagogy section of the website. The animation is simple, fluid, and predictable, which aids in observing movement and determining the direction of gaze.

🔹Diversify Interests: Preference Sequences

Two to three stimuli are presented one after the other at regular intervals—sounds, short videos, illuminated toys, musical excerpts, etc. The student's varied reactions to each stimulus are observed. If a marked interest is shown for a particular element, it is briefly repeated before continuing the sequence.

The activity can also be reversed: present stimuli that are already known and liked, then introduce a new element within the sequence to assess whether the student shows curiosity or interest in the addition. This is a flexible way to test openness to new interests while maintaining a high level of motivation.

This type of structured approach is often used in research with individuals with multiple disabilities because it allows preferences to be observed systematically and reproducibly. When adopting such an approach, it is essential to document observations rigorously and share the results with the team. This fosters a shared understanding of the student’s profile and enables interventions to be adapted in a coherent and concerted manner.
